Tchaikovsky's Fatum

Blog entry on 24 Nov 2019
Category: Music

A relatively unknown piece of music, and one of my favorite pieces, is Tchaikovsky’s Fatum, Op. 77 (published posthumously). Tchaikovsky composed the tone poem in 1868, very early in his career. While its premiere was perceived as a success, the second performance came with less delight. As Balakirev (to whom the piece is dedicated) wrote, “There wasn’t much applause … The seams show, as does all your clumsy stitching.” Tchaikovsky regretfully destroyed the score in response. Three years after Tchaikovsky’s death, researchers pieced together the orchestral parts to restore the score and published it.

There are snippets of Fatum in Tchaikovsky’s later works — he directly used the lyrical part in his opera The Oprichnik — but perhaps his work on Fatum indirectly influenced his future writings. I was listening to the Swan Lake finale recently when I was reminded of this poem. The use of the full brass section from 8 before rehearsal 21 to rehearsal 23 (the buildup before Odette’s death and Siegfried’s subsequent suicide) has parallels to sections of Fatum. The brassy chords accompanied by short descending phrases* right before rehearsal 21 share characteristics to many parts of Fatum, and other techniques such as building tension by repeating a note in different octaves appear in both Fatum and the finale. Combinations such as whole tones resolving to a major chord, or a moving brass line which pushes to the next phrase, also appear in both pieces.

*I’m not sure if this has a more official name, so I gave this a nickname of “sirens.”

At the conclusion of both works, Tchaikovsky throws a wrench into our ears by changing the main theme from minor to the parallel major, signifying a triumph over some evil force. In Fatum, Tchaikovsky seems to indicate that fate will drive everything towards a natural resolution, but the Swan Lake ending represents more of a resurrection of sorts as Odette and Siegfried ascend into the sky.

Perhaps this provides a different framework with which to analyze Swan Lake. Tchaikovsky’s frequently-used theme of fate seems to be embodied in the patterns that he uses repeatedly, especially the specific ways in which he uses brass. Maybe Tchaikovsky is indicating a subtle theme of fate lurking under the ballet?
